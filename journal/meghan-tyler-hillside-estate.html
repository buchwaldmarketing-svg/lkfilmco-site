Meghan + Tyler — The Hillside Estate

An October evening with golden light and real tears. A Dallas wedding story from behind the lens.

VenueThe Hillside Estate
DateOctober · Fall 2025
GuestsAbout 140
CoverageGetting-ready through reception

There are weddings where everything goes according to plan, and then there are weddings where something shifts — something real breaks through the timeline — and the film stops being a reenactment and starts being a record.

Meghan and Tyler's was the second kind.

The getting-ready hours

Meghan's bridal suite faces east at The Hillside Estate, which means the morning light is generous even on overcast days. She was ready 45 minutes before the ceremony — the kind of bride who did a first-look with her dad that turned into a full-body hug neither one of them wanted to end.

Her mom was in the corner, holding a handkerchief, trying not to be the one who cried first. She lost.

Tyler was with his groomsmen in the lower suite, pouring bourbon into paper cups and practicing vows in the mirror. He rewrote two lines that morning. You could tell he was nervous about getting it right.

The ceremony

Outdoors. Under the oak canopy at Hillside. 5 PM, which in October means the golden hour is already starting.

Meghan walked with her dad. She made it about ten steps before she started to cry. Tyler watched the whole aisle, jaw set, eyes wet. When her dad handed her off, they didn't exchange words — just a look that meant take care of her.

"I stopped lowering the camera during Tyler's vows. I just held it. He was shaking. That was the moment the film found itself."

Tyler read his vows first. He got through three lines before the paper started shaking in his hands. He laughed at himself, apologized to the crowd, and kept going. Meghan reached out and steadied his wrist. That's on film.

When Meghan started hers, she didn't need the paper. She looked at him the whole way through. I've filmed a lot of weddings, and that's not something you can rehearse.

The reception

The tent at Hillside faces west. The sunset timing was almost rude — that specific gold that photographers chase, unfolding during the first dance.

Their first dance was to a slow country song neither one of them had told me about ahead of time, so I didn't know the lyrics until I was two steps into the song with the camera rolling. The line that hit was "I'll build you a house out of everything I am" — and Tyler mouthed it along. Meghan had her forehead on his chest. The film has that on wide, close, and from behind. Three angles of one quiet moment.

Toasts were short and good. Meghan's maid of honor told a story from the fifth grade. Tyler's brother said he was proud of him. Nobody tried to be funny. Everybody landed.

They exited with sparklers, the usual, but somebody had wrapped their getaway car in white ribbon — Meghan's grandmother's ribbon, actually, from her grandmother's wedding in 1962. That detail I only learned later, when her mom told me during the final film delivery.

What stays with me

The best wedding films aren't the ones with the biggest budgets or the fanciest venues. They're the ones where something real happened and somebody happened to be there with a camera.

Hillside was beautiful. The light cooperated. But Meghan and Tyler brought the film. The rest of us just pointed lenses.
